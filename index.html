Passive Housing

So... What is it?

Definition

Passive housing is designed to reduce heat transfer, in order to reduce energy consumption from heating/cooling. That's where "passive," comes in; it regulates temperature through passive means, rather than traditional air conditioning or heating. Passive building design can go beyond "housing," design principles can be used for commercial or public structures as well.

Technology

  • Location - passive housing can be built in any location, but climate does matter
  • Taking advantage of the environment
    • Sun - prefer south-facing windows in cooler areas
    • Tree shading - plant trees for shade in hotter areas
  • Insulation - reduces thermal bridging (prevents overheating in summer and loss of heat in winter)
  • Windows/Doors - preserve air quality and reduce heat transfer
  • Ventilation - preserve air quality and controls moisture

Benefits

  • Lower energy costs for residents
  • Increased indoor air quality (Ventilation, less mold)
  • Resilience in power outages
  • Reduced maintenance footprints for the developers

Quick History

Passive house is a fairly new design paradigm.

  • First passive houses: Germany, 1990s (Dr. Wolfgang Feist)
  • At the beginning, mostly single-family homes (easier to prototype)

Considerations in the General Housing Design Process

Refurbishing

It is possible to remodel or renovate a traditional home to have passive features.

Integration with Other Technology

  • Solar energy
  • 3D printed concrete buildings
  • Green roofs

Density

Passive housing principles can be integrated into high-rise apartment complexes.

Housing Costs and Property Value

A passive house costs about 5-10% more to build than a traditional home.

Resale value is also expected to be higher than for traditional homes. But, context matters!

Organizations

Passive House Institute (PassivHaus Institut)

  • Founded 1996 by Dr Wolfgang Feist
  • Leader and pioneer in the passive house space; offers consulting to services to help organizations build better passive buildings
  • Rewards certifications to buildings that meet their standards (like Park Passive, in Seattle)
  • Hosts conferences and performs research about passive houses, to further their adoption

PHIUS

  • Phius = Passive House Institute, US
  • They set standards and certifications, including those for retrofited and commercial buildings. Also set standards for parts (like windows)!
  • PHIUS ZERO - Zero energy standard for newly constructed housing. Sets the net source energy target at ZERO, does not allow for fossil-fueled combustion on site, and provides both on-site and off-site renewable energy options to get to ZERO.
